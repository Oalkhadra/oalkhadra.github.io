Point-of-interest attribution under spatial and temporal uncertainty

USC InfoLab, with Dr. Cyrus Shahabi et al · 2024–2025

Attributing noisy GPS trajectories to candidate points of interest is a problem of inference under both spatial and temporal uncertainty: the device location is a distribution, the dwell window is fuzzy, and the set of plausible venues overlaps. Prior work spans heuristic nearest-centroid baselines, multi-stage kernel density methods, and integer-programming formulations with spatiotemporal priors, but the field lacked a reproducible comparison across these approaches.

My contribution was the benchmarking suite that made our transformer framework's results interpretable. I reconstructed two proprietary probabilistic baselines from limited documentation: SafeGraph's multi-stage KDE pipeline and Nishida's ILP formulation with spatiotemporal priors. I also built a fault-tolerant ETL pipeline processing 30M+ geospatial records to evaluate all methods on a common footing.

Decision-support modeling for drought policy under sector interdependence

GWU Systems Engineering, with Dr. Joost Santos et al · 2022–2023

Drought does not impact economic sectors in isolation: a shock to agriculture propagates through processing, transport, and downstream services in ways that linear damage estimates miss. The question we took up was how to quantify those cascading losses across California's 71 BEA sectors and evaluate which policy interventions actually reduce them.

Co-led the development of the California Drought Impact Assessment Tool (CDIAT), a dynamic inoperability input-output (DIIM) framework parameterized through expert elicitation from 32 subject-matter experts on sector-level disruption, recovery periods, and policy effectiveness. Ran sensitivity analysis across three mitigation strategies (conservation, wastewater recycling, and desalination) over multi-year recovery horizons; conservation reduced projected statewide losses by an estimated 61% (~$5.36B avoided) relative to the no-action baseline. Validated against documented losses from five historical California droughts spanning 1977–2022.

Probabilistic forecasting and prediction market calibration

Ongoing · trading live since February 2026 · working paper in preparation

Retail temperature prediction markets systematically over-price forecast uncertainty: market-implied dispersion exceeded realized dispersion by roughly 1.27×. That mispricing makes dispersion calibration the economically live quantity, and it motivates the system I built and operate: a pooled XGBoost forecast-error model spanning 20 U.S. cities and multiple NWP sources, with Student-t predictive distributions, retrained daily and trading Kalshi temperature contracts autonomously since February 2026.

The central research finding came out of the system's own diagnostics. The forecaster's pooled PIT histogram was statistically uniform, yet conditioning on an observable covariate revealed predictive dispersion mis-scaled by 25–40% across regimes: the pooled statistic was uniform only by cancellation. I documented three distinct mechanisms by which pooled calibration metrics mask conditional miscalibration, and deployed a one-line covariate-conditional variance scaling that corrects it in every regime under walk-forward evaluation and holds live. The decision layer applies distributionally robust trade selection and Kelly sizing (Lam 2016), with the ambiguity radius set from the forecaster's own calibration diagnostics.

Live results, measured on the system's own telemetry and frozen as of July 2026: across 10,757 market-bucket observations, the model's probabilities beat market-implied prices with a 10.3% Brier skill score and 12× lower expected calibration error (0.006 vs. 0.072). Across 1,000+ settled contracts, lifetime Sharpe of 2.2 (annualized from daily returns on deployed capital) and +39.1% return on risk under the current model, net of fees. These markets are thin, so the system is judged on calibration and process quality rather than absolute P&L.

Volatility forecasting and the statistical–economic significance gap

2025

A common assumption in applied volatility modeling is that more sophisticated forecasts should translate to better trading performance. I tested this directly: does XGBoost-based volatility forecasting produce measurable edge in a delta-neutral SPY options strategy relative to GARCH(1,1) and a naive 30-day rolling estimate?

Built a full backtesting framework on five years of SPY options data (2020–2025) with Black-Scholes pricing, regime-conditional variance risk premium estimation, mispricing detection, and daily delta rebalancing. Diebold-Mariano tests on QLIKE loss showed XGBoost significantly outperformed GARCH (p=0.004) with 21% lower RMSE, but neither model significantly beat the naive historical baseline (p=0.26). Despite the absence of statistical significance in forecasting accuracy, XGBoost generated $1.1M in option-only P&L across the backtest; nearly all of which was offset by hedge losses in a strongly trending market, leaving a modest +2.4% annualized return. The result isolates a real but underappreciated mechanism: forecasting accuracy can translate to better option selection without translating to strategy P&L, when hedging methodology dominates the path from signal to return.
